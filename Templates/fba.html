Amazon FBA Business Components

Amazon FBA (Fulfillment by Amazon) is a business model where you sell products, and Amazon handles storage, packing, shipping, and customer service. Sellers send inventory to Amazon warehouses, allowing for faster shipping (Prime) and easier scaling. It is used for private label, wholesale, and arbitrage, but requires management of inventory fees, which increase for long-term storage.

  • How It Works:You list products on Amazon, ship inventory to Amazon fulfillment centers, and they handle logistics, customer service, and returns.

  • Benefits: Access to Amazon Prime customers, expert logistics, and automated customer service.

  • Loan term—the amount of time over which the loan must be repaid in full. Most fixed-rate mortgages are for 15, 20, or 30-year terms. A shorter period, such as 15 or 20 years, typically includes a lower interest rate.

  • Costs & Fees:FBA fees include storage, fulfillment, and potentially high fees for products stored longer than 181 days.

  • Strategies: Many sellers focus on private labeling (creating a brand), wholesale, or retail/online arbitrage to source products.

  • Challenges:
    • Managing inventory levels to avoid high storage fees, competitive marketplace, and capital constraints.

About Us

Fulfillment by Amazon (FBA) is the easiest way for sellers to grow their online business. With FBA, you store your products in Amazon’s fulfillment centers, and Amazon takes care of the rest—packing, shipping, and even customer service. This means you can reach millions of customers, offer fast delivery through Amazon Prime, and focus on what matters most: growing your brand and expanding your product line. FBA gives sellers the freedom to scale their business without worrying about logistics, storage, or order management. Whether you’re just starting out or running a thriving online store, FBA empowers you to sell smarter, reach further, and provide a seamless shopping experience for your customers.

Costs Associated with Running an FBA Business

Monthly operating expenses usually make up the bulk of the costs involved in running an FBA business, but there are other important costs to consider. These costs can be separated into recurring and non-recurring categories.

Recurring Costs

This include ongoing expenses that occur regularly, such as storage fees, shipping and fulfillment, platform fees, and subscription services. These are essential for day-to-day operations and can affect your monthly cash flow.
Most recurring costs continue throughout the life of an FBA business and play a major role in overall profitability. These expenses are ongoing and can increase over time due to factors like business growth, storage duration, or changes in service rates. In the calculator, recurring costs can be included under optional settings, allowing for more accurate projections of monthly performance.

  • Fulfillment and shipping fees—these are the costs associated with picking, packing, and delivering products to customers. They vary depending on product size, weight, and destination. Faster delivery options or international shipping may increase these costs.
  • Storage fees—charges for keeping inventory in fulfillment centers. These fees are typically calculated based on the amount of space your products occupy and may increase during peak seasons or for long-term storage.
  • Platform fees—these are charges associated with selling on a marketplace, often calculated as a percentage of each sale. They can vary depending on product category and selling plan.
  • Returns and customer service handling—costs related to processing returned items and managing customer inquiries. These can fluctuate depending on product type and return rates. Advertising

Non-Recurring Costs

Non-Recurring Costs are one-time or occasional expenses, such as initial inventory purchases, equipment, software setup, or marketing campaigns. While these costs don’t happen every month, they can have a significant impact on your overall investment.

  • Initial inventory purchase—the cost of acquiring products to sell. Depending on your business strategy, this may include sourcing from suppliers, importing, or manufacturing. Large orders can require substantial upfront investment but are necessary to start selling.
  • Setup and equipment costs—expenses for setting up your FBA business, including packaging materials, labeling equipment, scanners, shelving, or other tools required for handling inventory. These are usually paid once but are critical for smooth operations.
  • Software and subscriptions—one-time purchases or annual fees for tools that help manage inventory, track sales, or optimize listings. While not monthly, these costs are often required when starting or scaling the business.
  • Marketing campaigns—initial promotional efforts to launch a product, attract customers, or generate brand awareness. This could include paid ads, influencer partnerships, or product giveaways.
